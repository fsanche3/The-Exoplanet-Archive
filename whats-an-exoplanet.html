What Is an Exoplanet?

An exoplanet is a planet outside of our solar system. Just like the planets in our system, there are various types like gas giants or super earths. So far in 2022, there has been around five thousand confirmed exoplanet discoveries since the first one dating back to 1992.


Astronomers and scientist alike learn about the compositions of these planets by measuring key components like the brightness or luminosity of a planet. This can tell them many details about the planet including the size or its orbit (if it has one).




Exoplanets come in all shapes and sizes and their compositions can be different from what were used to seeing. Astronomers have discovered planets made entirely of oceans. This isn’t so different from earth which is covered in about seventy percent of water. A lot of these planets have extreme climates, for example, on the exoplanet HD1897736 it rains glass sideways.

Exoplanet Types


Terrestrial

Earth sized planets or smaller composed of rock silicate, water, or carbon. Signs possible habituality such as oceans or atmospheres are likely but still being researched.

Super Earth's

Terrestrial or rocky planets larger than Earth but lighter than Neptune. Atmospheres are also a possibility on these planets.

Gas Giants

Their size resembles Saturn’s or Jupiter’s or even larger. So far most of these discoveries have been labeled ‘hot Jupiter’s’ which means that they orbit closer to the sun then Jupiter (in our solar system) does.

Neptunian

Like Neptune or Uranus in size. Likely to have a mixture of interior compositions but their atmospheres are largely helium and hydrogen and rocky cores.
